Art Gallery Problem

        Introduction

The art gallery problem is a problem in computational geometry where we look at an art gallery in the form of a polygon with n vertices and try to find out the minimum number of guards needed to watch the entire gallery. The most famous theorem derived for this problem is Chvátal’s theorem, where n/3 guards are always sufficient and occasionally necessary to cover a n-vertex polygon. In the following section we will give the proof of this theorem and also a brief discussion of the NP-hardness of the problem.

Proof

First, we set the base case as n=3, which is trivial as only one guard would be needed. Now we consider n>3 and use triangulation and vertex coloring to prove Chvátal’s theorem. We start by triangulating the entire polygon by drawing n-3 non-crossing diagonal edges between vertices. Then, we pick two of three colors to color vertices u, v that share an edge respectively, and assign the third color to the last vertex adjacent to edge uv. We repeat this process until no three vertices of each triangle in the polygon share a color. By induction, we know that because the whole polygon triangulated, each triangle has one vertex of a certain color. If vertices u and v of an edge uv are set to be the two of the three colors, then the third vertex that make up the triangle will always be the last color. Thus, we know that one of these colors will have at most n/3 vertices for a polygon with n vertices. Then, we will pick a color that has at most n/3 vertices and place the guards at the vertices of that color. We know that each triangle has a vertex of that color so the whole art gallery will be guarded if there is a guard at each of the vertices of that color. The problem was also proved to be NP-hard by 3SAT reduction in 1986.

Cultural Significance

The problem was significant in its influence in the field of computational geometry. Since the question was raised by Victor Klee in 1973, mathematicians and computer scientists have proposed algorithms and came up with efficient implementations continuously. The problem was also extended to more complex problems such as Chromatic Art Gallery Problem and Watchman Route Problem.

        Evaluation

Overall, we feel that using 3-coloring to prove the theorem that n/3 guards are always sufficient and sometimes necessary is very clever and straightforward. Originally, the proof was very complex. However, in 1973, Steve Fisk developed this proof using triangulation and it was much simpler than the other proofs. The proof that this problem is NP-hard is much more complex and would take more background knowledge and understanding to be able to fully comprehend, so we kept it very concise for the purpose of this project. To learn more about it, we recommend looking into this paper: https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/stamp/stamp.jsp?arnumber=1057165. It gives a much more detailed description and proof for those who would like to study it further.

Discussion

The art gallery algorithm has a wide range of real-word applications, such as sensor placement, lighting arrangement, and robotics. In 2001, CS Robotics Laboratory at Stanford University proposed a sensor placement strategy using art gallery algorithm. It also contributes a lot to other computational geometry problems, such as polygon partition and optimization problems. For further improvement, we found out that the 3-coloring solution has many shortcomings. First, it only considers the solution that places the guards on the vertices, which is not always the best solution. Second, it always works on non-convex polygons, but not convex polygons.
